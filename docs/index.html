About Investment Scout

Investment Scout is an automated research tool that scans the markets once a day and surfaces a short list of opportunities worth a closer look — each one with a plain-English explanation of why it showed up. It is a starting point for your own research, not advice and not a trading bot: it never buys or sells anything.

What it does

Every weekday after the US market closes, it scans US, ASX, and crypto markets for two kinds of opportunity: technical setups on the price chart (e.g. a cup-and-handle or a breakout) and fundamental strength in the business (a high-quality, reasonably-priced company in the Warren Buffett mould). It scores each one, keeps only those that clear a quality bar, and lists them here — and emails them to the owner.

Where the data comes from

All data is pulled from Yahoo Finance (via the free yfinance library): daily price & volume history, company financials, profile details, and institutional ownership. Because it's a free source, coverage is strongest for US large-caps, good for the ASX, and price-only for crypto. Data can occasionally be delayed or incomplete — so always treat a lead as a prompt to dig deeper, never as gospel.

How the scoring works

Each candidate gets a technical score (0–1) from chart pattern detectors and a fundamental score (0–1) from a Buffett-style screen of quality, moat, value, and management. These combine into a single Conviction score from 0–100. A name that looks strong on both the chart and the business gets a bonus and is flagged "High Conviction" — a great company and a sensible entry point. Only candidates above the bar are shown.

What it deliberately doesn't do

It doesn't place trades, hold your money, or connect to a broker. It doesn't predict the future or tell you what to buy. It can't see news, earnings calls, or anything beyond the numbers. Think of it as a tireless junior analyst that hands you a daily shortlist with its reasoning — the judgement, and the decision, stay with you.

Glossary

Our scores

Conviction
Our headline 0–100 confidence score, blending the chart (technical) and the business (fundamental) signals. When both line up, the score gets a bonus. Higher means a stronger lead — never a guarantee.
Strength
An overall read on how solid the business is, blending quality, moat, and value into one number — separate from whether now is a good time to buy.
Backing
How strongly professional investors back the company — mainly the share owned by institutions (funds like BlackRock and Vanguard), with a lift for insider ownership. High backing means the "smart money" is in.
Tiers: High Conviction / Technical / Fundamental
Technical = surfaced on the chart alone. Fundamental = surfaced on the business alone. High Conviction (both) = the chart and the business look strong at once — our top tier. Crypto is always technical-only.

Chart patterns (technical)

Cup-and-handle
A bullish pattern shaped like a teacup: a gradual, rounded decline and recovery (the cup), then a small dip and steadying (the handle), with the price ready to push higher. We require a genuinely rounded base — not a sharp V — and a price sitting just below its prior peak.
Breakout
The price pushes above a ceiling it has struggled to clear before (resistance), ideally on heavier-than-usual trading volume. Strong volume suggests real conviction behind the move rather than a fakeout.
Uptrend (moving averages)
The price is above its rising 50-day and 200-day average prices — a sign the broader direction is up. A "moving average" smooths out daily noise to show the underlying trend.
RSI (Relative Strength Index)
A 0–100 momentum gauge. Around 50 is neutral; we like healthy upward momentum (roughly 50–70) but treat very high readings (overbought) as a caution rather than a green light.
Resistance & volume
Resistance is a price level a stock has repeatedly failed to break above. Volume is how many shares traded — a surge alongside a move signals stronger participation.

Business metrics (fundamental)

Quality
How good the business is: consistently high return on equity, healthy profit margins, low debt, and positive free cash flow. Good money made without big risks.
Value
Whether the price looks reasonable for what you get — mainly the P/E ratio. A great company at a silly price is not a great investment.
Moat
A durable competitive advantage that protects profits — a strong brand, network effects, switching costs. We approximate it with steady, high margins and returns over time. Warren Buffett's favourite trait.
ROE (Return on Equity)
How much profit a company generates from shareholders' money each year. Consistently high ROE (say, above 15–20%) signals a genuinely productive, well-run business.
Profit margin
The share of revenue left as profit after costs. Fatter, stable margins point to pricing power and an efficient operation.
Debt-to-equity
How much the company borrows relative to its own capital. Lower is safer — heavily indebted companies are more fragile when conditions turn.
P/E ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
The share price divided by earnings per share — roughly, how many years of profit you're paying for. A lower P/E (for a solid business) suggests better value.
Free cash flow
The cash left over after a company pays its running costs and investment. Positive, growing free cash flow is the lifeblood of a healthy business.
Institutional & insider ownership
The share of a company held by big professional funds (institutions) and by its own executives and directors (insiders). High institutional holding shows professional confidence; insider holding shows skin in the game.
